03 — The setup

5 minutes. No code.

Two steps. First you install the Finance plugin (that unlocks all 10 skills). Then you plug in three GitHub links (that unlocks the three agents we actually want).

Step 1

Open Claude and go to Customize

Open Claude — web at claude.ai, or the desktop app. Bottom-left sidebar, click Customize. You'll need a paid plan for this: Pro, Max, Team, or Enterprise. Free tier won't cut it.

Step 2

Install the Finance plugin

Under Personal Plugins, hit the + button. Click Browse Plugins. Scroll until you see Finance. Add it.

Done. All 10 skills are now live in Claude.

Step 3

Add the three agents from GitHub

Back to Personal Plugins, hit + again. This time click Create Plugin → Add Marketplace → Add from a repository. Paste this link:

github.com/anthropics/financial-services/market-researcher

Do the exact same thing two more times, with these two links:

github.com/anthropics/financial-services/model-builder
github.com/anthropics/financial-services/earnings-reviewer

That's the whole install. You now have three institutional-grade agents living inside Claude. Open a new chat and just describe what you want — Claude picks the right one automatically.

Works everywhere. These agents run in Claude.ai (web), Cowork (desktop), and Claude Code (terminal). Same agents, same capabilities, pick whichever you like.
Agent 01

The Market Researcher.

One-line: it watches your portfolio 24/7 so you don't have to.

You paste in your tickers. It reads every news story, every SEC filing, every analyst rating change, every insider trade, every red flag — and it tells you what actually matters. Not a wall of links. Actual synthesis.

What it covers

  • News — filtered for what's material, not what's noisy
  • Analyst calls — upgrades, downgrades, and the reasoning behind them
  • SEC filings — 10-K, 10-Q, 8-K, translated to plain English
  • Sector shifts — industry moves that touch your holdings
  • Competitive intel — what your companies' rivals are doing
  • Risk flags — lawsuits, exec departures, regulatory heat

Three prompts to try

1. Monday morning briefing.

Here's my portfolio: [tickers]. Give me the Monday morning
briefing. What moved last week, what news dropped, any rating
changes, and — this is the important part — flag anything that
would make me reconsider owning any of these.

2. One-stock deep dive.

Give me a full report on [ticker]. Business, latest earnings,
analyst consensus, biggest risks, what the bulls are saying,
what the bears are saying, and whether the current price makes
sense given the numbers.

3. Sector map.

I'm thinking about the [sector] space. Map out the top 5-7
players. Who leads, who's gaining, who's dying. If I had to
pick one for a 2-year hold, who would it be and why.
Pro move. Set this up on a schedule. Ask Claude to run the portfolio briefing every Monday at 7am. You show up to a report, not a firehose.
Agent 02

The Model Builder.

One-line: it builds you a full Excel financial model on any stock, in the time it takes to make coffee.

This is the one that would've made me quit my job in a former life. Investment banks charge clients thousands for models like this. The Model Builder pulls the filings, projects revenue, forecasts expenses, runs a DCF, builds comparable analysis, throws in sensitivity tables, and hands you a downloadable .xlsx file. You open it in Excel and every assumption is a cell you can change.

What you get in the file

  • Income statement — history + 5-year projections
  • Balance sheet — assets, liabilities, working capital
  • Cash flow statement — operating, investing, financing
  • DCF valuation — spits out a fair value per share
  • Comparable analysis — trading multiples vs peers
  • Sensitivity tables — what the stock's worth if growth is 10% vs 30%
  • Assumptions tab — one page with every input you can tweak

Three prompts to try

1. Full build.

Build me a complete financial model for [ticker] in Excel.
3 years of history, 5 years of projections. Include a DCF, a
comp table, and sensitivity tables. Export it as .xlsx so I
can download it.

2. Quick fair-value check.

What's [ticker] actually worth? Build a fast DCF using current
consensus estimates. Show me the implied share price under
three cases — bull, base, bear.

3. Two-stock face-off.

I'm deciding between [ticker 1] and [ticker 2]. Build a
side-by-side model — revenue growth, margins, valuation
multiples, implied upside. Tell me which one is cheaper for
what you're getting.
Reality check. A model is a giant machine of assumptions. Claude gets the plumbing right — the formulas, the linkage, the math. But the assumptions (growth rate, margins, discount rate) drive the answer. Always open the assumptions tab, look at what it plugged in, and change anything that looks off before you trust the output.
Agent 03

The Earnings Reviewer.

One-line: it reads the earnings call so you don't have to sit through 90 minutes of corporate-speak.

Earnings calls are where companies tell you the truth about their business. But they bury it inside 45 pages of transcript and CFO-speak. The Earnings Reviewer reads the whole thing, pulls out what management actually said, flags what they avoided saying, tracks how their tone changed from last quarter, and tells you whether the reason you bought the stock still holds.

What it surfaces

  • Transcript breakdown — organized by topic, not by speaker
  • Guidance moves — did they raise, lower, or hold? By how much?
  • Tone shift — more or less confident than last quarter?
  • Key numbers — revenue, earnings, margins vs what the street expected
  • Red flags — dodged questions, vague answers, weird language
  • Thesis check — the reason you own it — does the call back it up?

Three prompts to try

1. One-call review.

Review the latest earnings call for [ticker]. Give me: the
numbers vs expectations, what management said about [specific
thing you care about], what changed in their guidance, and
anything I should be worried about.

2. The thesis test. (This is the one.)

I own [ticker] because I believe [your reason]. Read the
latest earnings call and tell me straight — does what
management said support that thesis or undermine it?
Be specific.

3. Earnings-season sweep.

This week, [ticker 1], [ticker 2], [ticker 3] all reported.
Give me a one-paragraph summary of each. Who beat, who missed,
whose guidance was ugly. Rank them from most bullish to most
bearish based on the *quality* of what they said, not just
the beat.
Pro move. Always tell it your thesis before you ask. "Review this call" gets you a summary. "Does this call support the reason I own the stock" gets you an answer.
04 — The chain

Chain all three in one prompt.

Where it stops being a tool and starts being a team.

Paste this one message and you get the kind of due diligence a small hedge fund runs before opening a position — a full research report on one stock, in about 15 minutes.

I'm thinking about buying [ticker]. Run full due diligence:

Step 1 — Market Researcher: news, analyst view, competitive
position, risks.

Step 2 — Model Builder: build me a DCF with bull/base/bear
cases and export the Excel.

Step 3 — Earnings Reviewer: read the last call. Any red
flags? Any tone shift?

Then give me your final call: buy, wait, or pass. And be
honest — if the numbers don't hold up, say so.

Other combinations worth running

  • Portfolio checkup. Paste your holdings, ask the Market Researcher to flag any position that's deteriorated, then have the Model Builder rebuild valuations on the weakest two.
  • Earnings prep. The night before a company you own reports, have the Model Builder run a scenario table and the Earnings Reviewer pull the questions analysts dodged last quarter.
  • New-idea hunting. Ask for 5 stocks under $50B market cap with revenue growth above 25% and improving margins that aren't already on everyone's radar. Then run full DD on the two most interesting.
05 — What nobody tells you

5 things that 10x the output.

1. Give it your thesis, not your ticker.

"Analyze NVDA" is a lazy prompt. "I own NVDA because I think AI infrastructure spend has 3 more years of 30% growth — does the data still support that?" is a real question. The output is 10x sharper.

2. Ask for the bear case out loud.

Claude defaults to balanced. If you want it to actually hunt for reasons to pass on a stock, tell it: "give me the strongest argument for not owning this right now." It will find them.

3. Always ask for the Excel.

With the Model Builder, always request the .xlsx export. A model you can open and tweak is worth 100x more than a summary you can't audit.

4. Batch, don't drip.

Paste your whole portfolio in one message. Claude will cover every position and flag what needs attention. This beats one-stock-at-a-time by a mile.

5. Verify the load-bearing numbers.

Claude will get 95% of it right. But if a specific number is going to drive your decision, open the actual SEC filing and check. The agent's job is to save you 20 hours of research — not to replace the last 10 minutes of judgment.

The one rule. These are research tools, not oracles. They analyze data, build models, and summarize calls. They cannot predict the future. Never invest based solely on AI output. Always do your own due diligence, and talk to a licensed advisor before making real money moves.
