§ 01 · The problem

People can smell AI writing.

And the second they smell it, they stop reading. Your post, your email, your cold outreach — dead on arrival, because it reads like a machine typed it.

It's never one big thing. It's a hundred tiny tells stacked up: the em dashes, the "it's not just X, it's Y," the words no human says out loud like delve, tapestry, testament, seamless, robust. Your brain clocks it in half a second even if you can't name why.

This matters most for cold email. Inboxes and readers are now allergic to AI-written copy. One whiff and you're spam. Sounding human isn't a nice-to-have — it's the difference between a reply and silence.

What the Humanizer does

It's a free skill built on Wikipedia's documented list of AI writing signs. You give it text. It:

  • Checks your writing against 33 specific AI tells
  • Rewrites it to sound like a real person wrote it
  • Runs a second pass on its own output to catch anything it missed
  • Can copy your voice if you feed it a sample of your writing
You don't need to be technical. No code, no terminal commands to memorize. If you can copy a link and paste it, you can install this and use it today.

Step 04 · the upgrade

Make it sound like you.

By default it cleans your text to a neutral "human" voice. But you can make it copy your voice. Feed it a sample of your own writing first:

/humanizer

Here's a sample of how I actually write:
[paste 2-3 paragraphs of your own real writing]

Now rewrite this in that same voice:
[paste the AI text you want fixed]

It studies your rhythm, your word choices, your quirks — then rewrites in that style instead of generic "clean." This is what turns a post from "fine" into "that's clearly Karem."

§ What it catches

The tells it kills for you.

You don't need to memorize these. The tool handles them. But seeing the list tells you exactly why your writing sounded off.

Words no human uses

delve, tapestry, testament, underscore, pivotal, vibrant, seamless, robust, leverage, showcase, foster.

The dead giveaway sentence shapes

"It's not just X, it's Y." The forced group of three. "Honestly? Here's the thing." Manufactured drama with clipped fragments.

The punctuation tic

Em dashes everywhere. It cuts them for periods, commas, or parentheses like a real writer would.

The filler and the fluff

"In order to," "due to the fact that," empty hype, "the future looks bright," and the chatbot sign-off "I hope this helps!"

33 patterns total, plus a second self-check pass. You paste, it cleans, it double-checks its own work. Done.
